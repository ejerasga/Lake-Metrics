Danao Lake

Lake Danao: Where Nature's Symphony Resides

Lake Danao, a distinctive guitar-shaped body of water situated on Leyte Island in the Philippines, has a rich history and ecological significance. Originally named Lake Imelda, it was officially designated as a national park on June 2, 1972, under the presidential memorandum of then-President Ferdinand Marcos. Subsequently, it was rebranded as Lake Danao National Park on February 3, 1998, following Proclamation No. 1155. Today, it benefits from the protection granted by Republic Act No. 7586, also known as the National Integrated Protected Area System (NIPAS) Act of 1992.



This beautiful lake serves as a vital source of potable water for at least seven towns in Eastern Leyte, including the city of Tacloban. Moreover, it plays a crucial role in irrigating the surrounding ricelands in municipalities such as Dagami, Burauen, Pastrana, and Tabontabon.



Lake Danao's volcanic origins are evident in its geological history. It is likely a graben or a depression formed by the active Philippine Fault (or Leyte Central Fault), which traverses the lake area in a north-south direction. The rock formations encompass andesitic volcanic rocks of Quaternary origin in the western side, andesitic and dacitic volcanics dating back to the Miocene era in the southeastern portion, and intermediate volcanic sediments of Quaternary origin in the northeastern part of the lake. Adjacent to the lake, wetlands can be found, which might be remnants of the lake's larger, more extensive past. Over time, sedimentation has transformed this portion of the lake into its current configuration.
